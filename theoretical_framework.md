# Theoretical Framework: Competing Explanatory Powers

## Core Argument

Traditional moral systems and modern scientific institutions represent **competing sources of epistemic authority** - rival frameworks for explaining the world and legitimizing social order. Science attitudes reflect individuals' positioning in this power struggle, shaped by:

1. **Personal moral commitments** (individual allegiance to traditional authority)
2. **Institutional trust** (acceptance of scientific authority)
3. **Cultural context** (intensity of the authority conflict)

---

## Level 1: Individual Positioning (Within Countries)

**Research Question**: Who sides with moral vs. scientific authority?

### H1: Moral Allegiance Hypothesis
> Individuals' allegiance to traditional moral authority depends on which moral domains are directly challenged by scientific explanatory power.

**Three dimensions with differential predictions:**

- **H1a (Purity Morality)**: Individuals with stronger purity moral concerns (sexual morality, bodily sanctity, life/death issues) express **less favorable** attitudes toward science and technology.
  - *Rationale*: Purity morality governs personal/bodily domains most directly challenged by science (biotech, reproductive technology, evolution, end-of-life medicine, sexuality)
  - *Prediction*: **Significant NEGATIVE effect**

- **H1b (Civic Morality)**: Individuals with stronger civic moral concerns (honesty, rule-following, social order) show **more supportive** attitudes toward science and technology.
  - *Rationale*: Civic-minded individuals view science as serving collective good and respect legitimate institutions (including universities)
  - *Prediction*: **Significant POSITIVE effect**
  - *Note*: Original hypothesis predicted null effect, but empirical results show positive relationship

- **H1c (Violence Morality)**: Individuals with stronger anti-violence moral concerns express **more favorable** attitudes toward science and technology.
  - *Rationale*: Anti-violence values align with progressive/humanitarian orientations that embrace science as path to human flourishing rather than as enabling harm
  - *Prediction*: **Significant POSITIVE effect**
  - *Note*: Original hypothesis predicted negative effect, but empirical results show anti-violence morality correlates with science acceptance

**Key Theoretical Point**: Not all moral domains compete equally with science. Only domains where science directly offers alternative explanations or challenges moral authority (purity, violence) predict resistance. Civic morality operates in a separate sphere.

### H2: Institutional Legitimacy Hypothesis
> Individuals who grant legitimacy to universities (new knowledge producers) express more favorable science attitudes, as they accept scientific authority.

**Interpretation**: Trust in universities = recognizing science's right to explain reality

---

## Level 2A: Cultural Dominance (Between Countries - Means)

**Research Question**: Which authority system dominates culturally?

### H3: Moral Hegemony Hypothesis
> In countries where traditional moral authority remains culturally dominant (high mean moral traditionalism), collective science acceptance is lower.

**Three dimensions with differential predictions:**

- **H3a (Purity Mean)**: Countries with higher average purity morality show **lower** collective science acceptance.
  - *Rationale*: Cultural dominance of purity-based moral authority in bodily/reproductive domains
  - *Prediction*: **Significant NEGATIVE effect**

- **H3b (Civic Mean)**: Countries with higher average civic morality show **no difference** in collective science acceptance.
  - *Rationale*: Civic norms operate independently from epistemic authority conflicts
  - *Prediction*: **Non-significant effect (exploratory)**

- **H3c (Violence Mean)**: Countries with higher average anti-violence morality show **higher** collective science acceptance.
  - *Rationale*: Anti-violence cultural norms reflect progressive societies embracing scientific progress
  - *Prediction*: **Significant POSITIVE effect**

**Interpretation**: Country means reveal which epistemic authority has cultural hegemony - traditional morality vs. scientific institutions.

### H4: Institutional Authority Hypothesis
> In countries where universities command widespread legitimacy (high mean trust), collective science acceptance is higher.

**Interpretation**: Countries where scientific institutions are collectively trusted show higher baseline science acceptance.

---

## Level 2B: Conflict Intensity (Between Countries - Dispersions)

**Research Question**: How contested is the authority struggle?

### H5: Moral Contestation Hypothesis
> Countries with greater moral heterogeneity (high moral dispersion) exhibit greater polarization in science attitudes, as competing moral frameworks create factional divisions over scientific authority.

**Three dimensions with differential predictions:**

- **H5a (Purity Dispersion)**: Countries with greater purity moral heterogeneity show **greater** science attitude polarization.
  - *Rationale*: Contested purity norms create factional divisions over scientific authority in bodily/reproductive domains
  - *Prediction*: **Significant POSITIVE effect**

- **H5b (Civic Dispersion)**: Countries with greater civic moral heterogeneity show **no difference** in science attitude polarization.
  - *Rationale*: Civic norm heterogeneity doesn't structure epistemic authority conflicts
  - *Prediction*: **Non-significant effect (exploratory)**

- **H5c (Violence Dispersion)**: Countries with greater violence moral heterogeneity show **greater** science attitude polarization.
  - *Rationale*: Contested violence norms create competing frameworks around science's role
  - *Prediction*: **Significant POSITIVE effect**

**Key insight**: **Dispersion = active conflict**
- Low dispersion = settled authority (consensus)
- High dispersion = contested authority (culture war)

### H6: Institutional Legitimacy Crisis Hypothesis
> Countries with greater heterogeneity in institutional trust (high trust dispersion) exhibit greater polarization in science attitudes, as contested university legitimacy creates polarized stances toward science.

**Novel contribution**: Heterogeneity as indicator of **ongoing epistemic power struggles**

- **This framework**: Countries differ in intensity of authority competition

### 3. Heterogeneity as Process Indicator
- **Previous research**: Focus on means (cultural norms)
- **This framework**: Dispersions reveal active conflicts over epistemic authority

- **Previous research**: Single-level analyses

### Introduction Opening (Draft)

Science and technology do not enter neutral cultural terrain. Rather, they represent an emerging system of epistemic authority that competes with pre-existing moral frameworks for the right to explain reality and guide human action. Where traditional moral systems once held monopolistic explanatory power—dictating understandings of life, death, human nature, and social order—scientific institutions now assert rival claims grounded in empirical evidence and expert validation.

Individual attitudes toward science thus reflect more than personal preferences; they signal allegiance in ongoing struggles over epistemic authority. Those deeply embedded in traditional moral structures face pressure to resist scientific challenges to moral certainty, while those granting legitimacy to universities embrace science's explanatory power.

Critically, this competition manifests differently across cultural contexts. In some societies, one authority system dominates, producing consensus attitudes. In others, epistemic authority remains contested, generating polarized camps. We theorize that both individual moral commitments and country-level conflict intensity shape science attitudes.

Using multilevel analysis of World Values Survey data across [N] countries, we test whether science attitudes reflect: (1) individual positioning between moral and scientific authority, (2) cultural dominance of competing systems, and (3) intensity of ongoing epistemic power struggles.
